The Film

A documentary short for the decade nobody was looking.

A five-minute film in which three Marketwake humans — including Brooke MacLean in the most honest interview she has ever given — redefine what the last ten years actually were, and what the next ten years are for.

The film is not a retrospective. It is a public declaration of what Marketwake became by the time the rest of the world was paying attention to something else.

It is shot in the visual language of contemporary documentary — cinematic interview confessions, verité office footage, archival grain, patient cutting. No voiceover narration from the outside. No client logo reel. No agency-speak. Every voice in the film is a voice from inside the room.

Most agency films are made for the agency. This one is for the client who has been quietly disappointed by three agencies in a row and no longer believes any of them care.

References

  • Full Swing (Netflix) — structural backbone and interview language
  • The Last Dance — archival treatment and intimate interview framing
  • Abstract: The Art of Design — confidence in silence
  • Apple's anniversary films — craft-first emotional restraint

Tone

  • Observational, not promotional. Nothing in the film says Marketwake is great. The film makes you decide.
  • Warm, not glossy. Practical lighting. Real rooms.
  • Patient. Silence is a tool. One central shot holds without a cut for more than thirty seconds.
  • Confident. Ten years earns a swagger — but the swagger is quiet.
Structure

Cold open. Four chapters. Close.

Total runtime: approximately five minutes.

Cold Open · 0:00–0:30

The Marketwake office at night. Handheld. No names. No music until the title punches in. The posture is set: this film will show you, not tell you.

Chapter 01 · THE BOAT · 0:30–1:45

Brooke MacLean in interview, intercut with archival footage from 2016 and the earliest years of the company. The origin story — followed by the single moment the whole rest of the film is built around.

Chapter 02 · THE HIVE · 1:45–2:45

Two additional Marketwake voices introduced. Verité office footage. The culture of the house — delivered by the people inside it, in their own words.

Chapter 03 · THE WORK · 2:45–3:30

Proof of life. Actual client work mid-creation. Milestone cards earned by the pacing. Closes with Brooke's quietest line of the film.

Chapter 04 · JUST ONE · 3:30–4:40

Brooke's declarative register. Three manifesto lines, spaced by the other voices echoing them in their own language. Builds to the film's emotional landing.

Close · 4:40–5:15

Office exterior at dusk. Saint-Exupéry. Title card. Optional post-credit beat — two seconds of the team laughing, cut to black mid-laugh.

The Cast

Three voices. One room.

Brooke MacLean · Founder & CEO · Ten Years

The spine of the film. Carries the origin story, the central admission moment, and the manifesto register at the close. Tightest interview framing. Warmest lighting. Most patient cuts.

Character 2 · Long-Tenured Team Member · Seven Years

The middle-era voice. Represents craft and the internal culture of pushing work until it is dangerous. In Chapter 4, gets one line that echoes Brooke's manifesto — but in their own language, not hers.

Character 3 · The Newer One · Two to Four Years

The next decade. Represents forward motion. In Chapter 4, gets one line that echoes the film's final emotional landing — again, in their own words.

Optional Fourth · A Client

Eight seconds maximum. A long-tenured client confirming what the film has already shown. Used only if the line is perfect. Cut if it is merely good.

The Moment

THE ADMISSION

A single unbroken shot in Chapter 1. Approximately thirty to forty seconds. The camera does not move. No cutaways. No music. Just Brooke, and a question, and the silence around her answer.

Every film that changes a viewer's life contains a moment the subject was not supposed to give. Jordan admitting the loneliness. Tiger in a hospital bed. Jony Ive admitting failure. Those moments are not decoration — they are the structural foundation that makes everything else the film claims feel true.

For WAKE to land as a legacy artifact rather than a brand film, Brooke needs to say something on camera she has never said to the team. That is what this moment is for.

If you could go back to 2016 Brooke — not the pep-talk version — what would you actually tell her?

The question works because it bypasses the founder's usual defenses. It does not ask about hardship directly, which produces rehearsed answers. It asks her to imagine speaking to a version of herself who did not yet know what she knows now — which is emotionally loaded in a way that is difficult to fully armor against.

How it gets shot

  • Locked-off camera. A-cam only. No B-roll pre-shot for cutaway cover.
  • The interviewer is off-camera, speaks softly, asks the question, and then says nothing else. No follow-ups until she is done.
  • If she does not answer in the first fifteen seconds, we wait. The silence is the shot.
  • After she answers, we hold another five seconds on her face before cutting. The reaction is part of the line.

How it gets cut

  • We do not cover the emotional beats with b-roll. Staying on her face is the point.
  • No music underneath. Silence carries it.
  • The cut out is to an archival image — a photo of the early team, or the first office — not to another interview.

Preparation

The pre-interview conversation.

An operational document for the person who will prepare Brooke to be honest in the real interview. Its job is not to rehearse her. Its job is to loosen her.

Who runs it

The director, or a trusted Marketwake person who is not in Brooke's normal daily rhythm. Not her assistant. Not her second-in-command. Someone she can be tired around.

When and how

Four to seven days before the shoot. Sixty to ninety minutes. Coffee or a walk — hot a conference room. Not recorded. Not a pep talk. Off the record.

Phase 1 · Warm-up (10–15 minutes)

“Tell me about this week at work. What's taken up the most headspace?”

“When you picture the office in your head right now — whose face do you see first?”

The goal is to get her out of founder-speak and into specific, small, recent detail. Notice what she lights up about. Follow it.

Phase 2 · Origin (15–20 minutes)

“What was the physical space where Marketwake started — the actual room, the lighting, the chairs?”

“Who was in that room with you?”

“What's one stupid thing you bought or did in year one that you still think about?”

Anchor her in embodied memory, not strategic narrative. Embodied memory produces the best interview answers.

Phase 3 · The middle years (20–25 minutes)

“What year was hardest? Don't tell me why yet — just tell me the year.”

“What was happening that year that the team didn't know?”

“Was there a moment you thought about stopping?”

“Who did you call? Or did you not call anyone?”

This is the zone the admission comes from. Not today — but her subconscious will work on it for the next four days. That is the point.

Phase 4 · The self-address (10–15 minutes)

“If you could walk up to 2016 Brooke right now and put a hand on her shoulder, what would you tell her?”

“Not the version you'd say in a keynote. The version you'd say if it was just the two of you.”

If she goes to pep-talk, gently: “Okay — but what's the thing you wouldn't say on stage?”

The most important phase. The last thing you do. She does not need to land on her real answer today. She needs to let the question get under her skin.

How the conversation ends

When the Phase 4 questions are out, stop. Do not process. Do not summarize. Do not tell her the questions she will be asked on camera. Change the subject. Let her leave without a homework assignment.

The only thing you say on the way out: “That question about 2016 Brooke — I'm going to ask it again on camera. You don't need an answer ready. Just let it sit.”

A note to the person running this

Brooke is a skilled communicator. Her instinct is to say things well. For this one conversation, your job is to invite her to say things badly — in fragments, with pauses, without the sentence landing. That is where the real material is. If at any point she says “I don't know how to say this” — stop. That is the moment. Write it down exactly. Ask her to say it the same way on camera.

Production

The look.

The interview room

One designed space reused for all three subjects. Dark walls or a controllable backdrop. A warm practical lamp in frame. The subject's chair. An off-camera soft light. This can be a dressed conference room, a rented photo studio, or a corner of the office with a controlled window. The reuse is non-negotiable — matching visual language is what makes the chapters feel like a series.

Camera and lens

  • Cinema camera — Sony FX6, FX3, or Red Komodo. Not a mirrorless.
  • A-camera tight (50mm or d5mm) on the subject's face. B-camera wide for cutaway inserts.
  • Shallow depth of field at f/2 or f/2.8. Background falls off.
  • Interview frames locked off. Verité footage handheld. The distinction matters.

Lighting

  • Single-source key — Litemat or Aputure softbox — 45° off-axis, 3200K.
  • Practical lamp in frame for depth.
  • Negative fill on the shadow side. Nothing is fully filled in.
  • Optional subtle haze.

Audio

  • Wireless lav and boom backup on every subject.
  • Room mic for ambient capture during interviews.
  • Every interview records a minimum of thirty minutes, even if thirty seconds is used.

Wardrobe

  • Each subject wears their own clothes. No logos. No stylist overlay.
  • Pre-brief: “Wear what makes you feel like yourself at work.”
Archival

The footage the film depends on.

The single biggest production risk is weak archival. The team-wide hunt begins in Week 1.

Priority list

  • Earliest known photo of the Marketwake team — founding-era, 2016
  • Early logo sketches, naming documents, year-one whiteboards
  • Brooke in early context, ideally with fewer than five people around her
  • Handwritten notes, rough pitch materials, the first workspace
  • Press clippings and award announcements — Inc. 5000, Best Places to Work

Where to find it

  • Brooke's personal phone and cloud — usually the best source for founding-era photos
  • Original team members via a team-wide Slack request
  • Archived Google Drive and Dropbox folders from pre-2020
  • Wayback Machine captures of marketwake.com from 2016–2018
  • Investor and award-submission decks with early history references

Work to feature

  • Yamaha — Women Run the Water
  • Junk.com brand identity
  • Retention.com rebrand
  • Cutting Edge Firewood
  • Crunchafi (formerly LeaseCrunch)
Sound

Music, design, type.

Score direction

One anchor track that evolves across the runtime — hot score-for-every-beat. Licensed via Musicbed or Marmoset. Custom score only if budget allows.

References

  • Khruangbin — Pelota, White Gloves
  • Bonobo — Kerala, Cirrus
  • Labrinth — score work from Euphoria
  • Ólafur Arnalds — re:member
  • Hans Zimmer — Time

Sound design rules

  • Cold open: silence and diegetic office sound only.
  • Chapter transitions: hard cut to black, a quarter-second of silence, then title card and a single low hit.
  • Verité sections: real audio bleed, mixed low but always present.
  • Interview sections: faint ambient room tone under every line.
  • The admission: no music. Silence carries it.
  • The manifesto: no music during Brooke's three declarative lines. Music swells only on “just one away.”
  • Close: music resolves, office ambience fades, one full second of black before the end card.

Typography

  • Chapter cards: single words, white sans on black, 80pt and up, generous tracking.
  • Lower-thirds: small, bottom-left. NAME / TITLE / TENURE.
  • Final card: MARKETWAKE / 2016 →. Nothing else.
  • Typeface: Marketwake brand display, or Söhne / Neue Haas Grotesk / GT America.

  • Explicit CTA on the end card.
Risks

What could break this — and what we do about it.

The primary risk

The admission does not happen. Brooke stays in founder mode and Chapter 1 does not have its beating heart. The mitigation is the pre-interview conversation. The accepted fallback: shoot the rest of the film as-is, redesign Chapter 1 as a pure archival-and-origin story. The film still exists — just at a lower ceiling.

Secondary risks

  • Characters 2 and 3 cannot speak naturally on camera. Pre-interview them the week before. If still stiff, recast.
  • Archival is weak. Kill aggressively in Week 1 via team Slack. The retrospective collapses without it.
  • Verité feels staged. Give the team 24 hours' notice, not two weeks.
  • Runtime creeps past 5:30. Hard cut at 5:15 in the edit room, even if it hurts.
  • Music licensing is the most common final-week delay. Clear it in Week 2, not Week 8.

Guardrails

  • No stock footage. Period.
  • No lens-to-camera reads. All interview framing is observational.
  • No client logo reel. Work on screen is the making, not the hero shot.
  • No revenue figures. No headcount. The thesis is that houses are not measured that way.
  • No agency-speak. No synergy, no partner, no thought leadership, no impact.
  • The admission shot is not covered with b-roll. If it is shot, we commit to holding.
A Note

What this film is for.

Most agency anniversary films are made for the agency. They play at the holiday party. They get pinned to the homepage. They live on an employee onboarding playlist. They never actually change anyone's mind about anything.

This film is built differently. It is not for Marketwake.

It is for the client who has been quietly disappointed by three agencies in a row and no longer believes any of them care. It is for the senior creative at another firm who has been thinking about leaving but is not sure where to go. It is for the intern who just got hired and is trying to understand why she picked this place over the others. It is for the version of Brooke who, four years from now, will need to remember why she built it.

The measure of whether this film worked will not be Instagram views. It will be the number of people who, after watching, pick up the phone.

Make something true. The rest takes care of itself.
